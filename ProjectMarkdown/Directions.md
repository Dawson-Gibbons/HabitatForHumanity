### **HVAC**

**1\. Furnace Filter Replacement**

* **Locate the Slot:** Find the filter service door on the side or bottom of your furnace unit—it’s usually a narrow slot where the return air duct meets the main cabinet.  
* **Check the Size:** Slide the old filter out and look at the dimensions printed on the side (like 16x25x1) so you buy the perfect match.  
* **Note the Arrow:** Look for the "Airflow" arrow on the new filter; it must point **toward** the furnace and away from the return ducts.  
* **Slide and Secure:** Slide the fresh filter into the slot, making sure it’s flush and not crumpled, then pop the cover back on.  
* **Mark the Date:** Use a sharpie to write the date on the edge of the filter; you should be swapping these out every 3 months to keep the motor happy.

**2\. Thermostat Basics**

* **Check the Batteries:** If the screen is blank or acting glitchy, pop the faceplate off and swap the AA or AAA batteries—most people forget they even have them\!  
* **Level and Dust:** Ensure the unit is level on the wall (tilted thermostats can give wonky readings) and use a soft brush to whisk away dust from the internal sensors.  
* **Set a Schedule:** Program your "Home," "Away," and "Sleep" settings to save energy; aim for a 7- to 10-degree difference when you’re out of the house.  
* **Mind the Placement:** Ensure no lamps, TVs, or sunny windows are right next to the thermostat, or it’ll think the whole house is a sauna.  
* **The "Fan" Setting:** Keep the fan on "Auto" so it only runs when heating or cooling; "On" runs it 24/7, which is great for air filtration but expensive for your bill.

**3\. Air Vents \- Cleaning and Unblocking**

* **Clear the Path:** Walk through every room and make sure no rugs, curtains, or fabulous furniture are blocking your supply or return vents.  
* **Remove the Grille:** Use a screwdriver to remove the vent covers from the floor, wall, or ceiling so you can get deep into the "drama."  
* **Vacuum the Boots:** Use a shop vac or a long hose attachment to suck out the dust, pet hair, and "mystery crumbs" lurking in the first few feet of the ductwork.  
* **Wash the Vents:** Take the metal covers to the sink and scrub them with warm, soapy water to remove the sticky film that traps dust.  
* **Adjust the Louvers:** Once reinstalled, ensure the louvers are open and angled to distribute air toward the center of the room, not just hitting the wall.

**4\. HVAC: When to Call a Pro**

* **Listen for Drama:** If your unit is screeching, grinding, or thumping, shut it down immediately; those are "call a professional" sounds.  
* **Check the Air:** If the air coming out of the vents isn't ice-cold (AC) or toasty (Heat), you likely have a refrigerant leak or a dead compressor.  
* **Sniff Around:** Any "electric" burning smells or gas odors near the furnace are immediate red flags for a technician.  
* **Short Cycling:** If the unit turns on and off every few minutes without reaching the temp, your thermostat or sensors are having a meltdown.  
* **Puddles:** Water pooling around an indoor unit usually means a clogged condensate line or a cracked drain pan that needs expert eyes.

### **Plumbing**

**5\. Water Main Shutoff**

* **Locate the Valve:** Find your main water line where it enters the house—usually in the basement, a crawlspace, or a utility closet near the front of the foundation.  
* **Identify the Type:** Look for either a **Gate Valve** (a round wheel handle) or a **Ball Valve** (a straight lever handle).  
* **The Big Twist:** For a lever handle, turn it 90 degrees so it’s perpendicular to the pipe; for a wheel, turn it clockwise until it stops.  
* **Release the Pressure:** After shutting it off, go to the lowest faucet in the house and turn it on to drain the remaining water out of the pipes.  
* **Clear the Area:** Make sure there isn't any clutter or "storage drama" blocking the valve so you can reach it in a split second during an emergency.

**6\. Unclog Drains**

* **Plunge First:** Use a cup plunger for sinks and create a tight seal; give it several vigorous heaves to break the clog.  
* **Zip It Up:** Insert a plastic drain snake (the ones with the little teeth) to pull out hair clogs—it’s gross, but so satisfying.  
* **Baking Soda & Vinegar:** Pour half a cup of baking soda followed by half a cup of vinegar; let it fizz for 15 minutes to dissolve organic gunk.  
* **Check the P-Trap:** Place a bucket under the sink, unscrew the U-shaped pipe, and clear out whatever is stuck inside manually.  
* **Boiling Water:** Finish with a kettle of boiling water to flush out any remaining oils or soap scum (but only if you have metal pipes\!).

**7\. Fix a Running Toilet**

* **Check the Chain:** Ensure the lift chain isn't too long (getting caught under the flapper) or too short (keeping it propped open).  
* **Test the Flapper:** Push down on the rubber flapper at the bottom; if the hissing stops, the flapper is old and needs a $5 replacement.  
* **Adjust the Float:** If water is pouring into the overflow tube, turn the adjustment screw on the float arm to lower the water level.  
* **Clean the Seat:** Feel the rim where the flapper sits; if it’s slimy or gritty, wipe it down so the seal is airtight.  
* **Replace the Fill Valve:** If the water won't stop and the float is fine, the fill valve is toast—clip on a new one and reconnect the tube.

**8\. Undersink Valves**

* **Locate the Handles:** Look for the small oval handles under your sink; the one on the left is usually hot, and the right is cold.  
* **The "Righty-Tighty" Rule:** Turn the handle clockwise until it stops to shut off the water; don't yank it, just a firm twist.  
* **Test the Tap:** Open the faucet above to ensure no water is coming out; this confirms the valve actually works.  
* **Loosen with Care:** If the valve is stuck, spray a little WD-40 on the stem and wait ten minutes before trying again gently.  
* **Check for Leaks:** After turning it back on, wipe it with a dry tissue; if it gets wet, tighten the packing nut behind the handle slightly.

**9\. Water Heater Maintenance**

* **Turn Off Power:** Flip the breaker for electric heaters or turn the gas knob to "Pilot" before you start playing with water.  
* **Connect a Hose:** Attach a standard garden hose to the drain valve at the bottom and run the other end to a floor drain or outside.  
* **Open the Valve:** Open the drain valve and a hot water faucet upstairs to break the vacuum and let the water flow.  
* **Flush the Sediment:** Let it run until the water looks crystal clear; this removes the "calcium crunchies" that make it inefficient.  
* **Refill & Power Up:** Close the drain valve, let the tank fill completely *before* turning the power back on (or you'll fry the heating element\!).

**10\. Prevent/Thaw Frozen Pipes**

* **The Drip Method:** When it's freezing, leave your furthest faucet on a tiny, pencil-lead-thin drip to keep water moving.  
* **Open the Cabinets:** Open the doors under your sinks to let the warm air from the room reach the "shy" pipes hiding in the walls.  
* **Identify the Freeze:** If no water comes out of the tap, leave the faucet open so pressure can escape as the ice thaws.  
* **Apply Gentle Heat:** Use a hair dryer or a heating pad on the frozen section—never use a torch unless you want a house fire.  
* **Work Toward the Tap:** Always start thawing from the faucet side and work your way back toward the main so the melting water has an exit.

### **Electrical**

**11\. Master the Breaker Box**

* **Find the Panel:** Locate your main service panel (usually in the garage, basement, or a closet) and open the metal door.  
* **Identify the Trip:** Look for a switch that isn't fully "On" or "Off"—it’ll be hanging out in the middle, sometimes showing a red or orange sliver.  
* **Reset the Switch:** Flip that moody breaker all the way to "Off" until it clicks, then flip it firmly back to "On."  
* **Label Everything:** Use a permanent marker or a label maker to name each circuit so you aren't guessing which switch kills the kitchen lights.  
* **Check for Heat:** If a breaker feels hot to the touch or smells like a burnt circuit board, leave it off and call an electrician immediately.

**12\. GFCI Outlets (The Ones with Buttons)**

* **Locate the Buttons:** Find the "Test" and "Reset" buttons on outlets near water (kitchens, bathrooms, or outdoors).  
* **Press "Test":** Push the test button; you should hear a "snap," and anything plugged into it should lose power immediately.  
* **Press "Reset":** Push the reset button back in until it clicks to restore the flow of juice to the outlet.  
* **Test Monthly:** These little lifesavers can fail silently, so make it a habit to click through them once a month.  
* **Replace if Stubborn:** If the button won't stay in or doesn't "snap" when tested, the internal safety mechanism is dead—time for a new outlet.

**13\. Replacing a Light Switch**

* **Kill the Power:** Go to your breaker box and flip the switch for that room; try the light to make 100% sure it’s dead.  
* **Remove the Plate:** Unscrew the decorative cover and the two screws holding the switch to the wall box, then gently pull it out.  
* **Label the Wires:** Take a photo or use tape to mark which wire goes to which screw (usually a black "hot" wire and a white "neutral").  
* **Swap the Switch:** Loosen the screws, move the wires to the same positions on your new switch, and tighten them down securely.  
* **Tuck and Screw:** Gently fold the wires back into the box, screw the switch into place, and pop the cover back on.

**14\. Electrical: When to Call a Pro**

* **Flicker & Fade:** If your lights dim whenever the fridge kicks on, your home’s wiring is struggling to handle the load.  
* **The "Bzzz" Factor:** Any humming, buzzing, or sizzling sounds coming from your walls or outlets are a major fire risk.  
* **Discolored Plates:** If an outlet cover looks charred, melted, or discolored, the wires behind it are overheating.  
* **Sparking:** A tiny blue spark when plugging something in is okay, but a big yellow pop or smoke means you've got a short.  
* **Aluminum Wiring:** If your home was built in the late 60s/early 70s and has aluminum wiring, have an expert check the connections.

### **Exterior**

**15\. Cleaning the Gutters**

* **Ladder Safety:** Use a sturdy extension ladder and have a friend hold the base; never lean out too far to the side.  
* **Scoop the Gunk:** Use a plastic gutter scoop or an old spatula to clear out leaves and "roof sludge" into a bucket.  
* **Flush the Line:** Take a garden hose and spray water toward the downspouts to clear out any remaining fine debris.  
* **Clear the Downspouts:** If water backs up, your downspout is clogged; spray the hose up from the bottom to break the "clump."  
* **Check the Slope:** Ensure the gutters aren't sagging; water should always flow toward the downspouts, not sit in puddles.

**16\. Caulking Windows**

* **Prep the Surface:** Use a putty knife to scrape away old, peeling caulk and wipe the area clean with a damp rag.  
* **Cut the Tip:** Cut the nozzle of your caulk tube at a 45-degree angle—the smaller the hole, the cleaner your bead will be.  
* **The Steady Pull:** Hold the caulk gun at a 45-degree angle and pull it steadily along the gap while applying even pressure.  
* **The Wet Finger Trick:** Dip your finger in water (or use a smoothing tool) and run it over the bead to press it into the gap.  
* **Seal the Gaps:** Check both the top and sides of the window frame; keeping air out saves you a fortune on your electric bill.

**17\. Roof Basics (Inspection)**

* **The Ground View:** Use binoculars to look for missing, cracked, or "curling" shingles without ever leaving the grass.  
* **Granule Check:** Check your gutter runoff; if you see tons of sandy "shingle crumbs," your roof is losing its protective layer.  
* **Flash Inspection:** Look at the metal "flashing" around chimneys and vents; if it’s rusted or gapping, that’s where leaks start.  
* **Sag Check:** Stand back and look at the ridgeline; if the roof looks like it’s "dipping," you may have structural moisture damage.  
* **Inside the Attic:** During a heavy rain, go into the attic with a flashlight to look for damp rafters or pinpricks of light.

**18\. Driveway Repair (Asphalt)**

* **Clean the Crack:** Use a wire brush and a leaf blower to get every bit of dirt and grass out of the crack.  
* **Apply Filler:** For thin cracks, use a liquid "crack filler"; for holes, use a "cold patch" compound from a bag.  
* **Tamp it Down:** Use a heavy tamper or the end of a 4x4 post to pack the patch material down until it’s rock hard and level.  
* **Let it Cure:** Give the patch at least 24 hours (or whatever the label says) before driving your car over it.  
* **Seal the Deal:** Every few years, roll on a fresh coat of driveway sealer to protect the surface from UV rays and oil spills.

**19\. Grading & Drainage**

* **The 10-Foot Rule:** Ensure the ground slopes away from your house at a rate of 1 inch per foot for the first 10 feet.  
* **Fill the Low Spots:** Use "clean fill" dirt (not mulch\!) to build up areas where water puddles against your foundation.  
* **Extend Downspouts:** Add plastic extensions to your downspouts so they discharge water at least 5 feet away from the house.  
* **Check the Swales:** If your yard has a natural "valley" (a swale), keep it clear of debris so it can funnel water to the street.  
* **Watch the Rain:** During a storm, grab an umbrella and walk around to see exactly where the "rivers" are forming in your yard.

### **Lawn & Garden**

**20\. The Lawn Calendar**

* **Early Spring Wake-up:** Apply a pre-emergent herbicide before the soil hits 55°F to stop crabgrass before it even thinks about sprouting.  
* **Late Spring Feeding:** Use a slow-release fertilizer to give your grass the nutrients it needs for the summer "growth spurt."  
* **Fall Aeration:** Rent a core aerator in September to pull "plugs" from the soil; this lets oxygen and water reach the roots.  
* **Overseeding:** Right after aerating in the fall, spread new seed over thin spots to keep the lawn thick and lush for next year.  
* **Winter Dormancy:** Mow one last time on a lower setting in late fall and clear all leaves so they don't suffocate the grass over winter.

**21\. Mowing Like a Pro**

* **Height Matters:** Set your mower deck high (usually 3–4 inches); taller grass has deeper roots and shades out those pesky weeds.  
* **The 1/3 Rule:** Never trim more than one-third of the grass height at a time, or you’ll "shock" the lawn and turn it yellow.  
* **Keep it Sharp:** Sharpen your mower blades every season; dull blades "shred" the grass, making it prone to disease.  
* **Alternate Patterns:** Change the direction you mow each time (North-South then East-West) to prevent the grass from leaning and soil from compacting.  
* **Leave the Clippings:** Unless they are clumping, leave the grass clippings on the lawn—they act as a free, natural fertilizer\!

**22\. Strategic Watering**

* **Early Bird Special:** Water between 4 AM and 10 AM so the grass dries before evening, preventing fungus and mold from moving in.  
* **Deep and Infrequent:** Aim for 1 inch of water per week in one or two long sessions rather than a little bit every day.  
* **The Tuna Can Test:** Place an empty tuna can on the lawn; once it’s full, you’ve hit your 1-inch goal for the week.  
* **Check the Soil:** Stick a screwdriver into the ground; if it goes in easily, you’re hydrated. If it’s a struggle, your lawn is thirsty.  
* **Dormancy is Okay:** During extreme heat, it’s fine to let the lawn go "brown" and dormant; it will bounce back when the rain returns.

**23\. Mulching Magic**

* **Clear the Area:** Pull all weeds and rake away old, matted mulch before you start spreading the fresh stuff.  
* **Edge the Bed:** Use a spade to cut a clean, 3-inch deep "trench" around the garden bed to keep the mulch from creeping onto the grass.  
* **Two-Inch Rule:** Spread a layer about 2 to 3 inches thick; any thinner and weeds get through, any thicker and you’ll choke the plants.  
* **No Volcanoes:** Keep mulch a few inches away from tree trunks and stems; piling it against the bark causes rot and invites "critters."  
* **Smooth it Out:** Use a hard rake to level the surface, then lightly water the mulch to settle it and keep it from blowing away.

**24\. Winterizing Faucets**

* **Disconnect Hoses:** Remove all garden hoses and sprayers; if you leave them attached, trapped water will freeze and burst the pipe.  
* **Drain the Line:** If you have an indoor shut-off valve for the outdoor tap, turn it off and open the outdoor faucet to drain any remaining water.  
* **Check the Drip:** Ensure the faucet is fully closed and not dripping; even a tiny leak can create a massive ice block inside your wall.  
* **Install the "Hat":** Place an insulated foam cover (the "Styrofoam igloos") over the faucet and tighten the loop to keep it cozy.  
* **Storage:** Roll up your hoses and store them in the garage or basement so the rubber doesn't crack in the freezing temps.

### **Interior Maintenance**

**25\. Patching Drywall**

* **Clean the Hole:** Trim away any frayed paper or loose drywall bits with a utility knife to create a clean "crater."  
* **Apply the Patch:** For small dents, use spackle; for holes, apply a self-adhesive mesh patch over the opening.  
* **The First Coat:** Use a putty knife to spread a thin layer of joint compound over the patch, "feathering" the edges outward so it’s flat.  
* **Sand it Smooth:** Once dry, use a fine-grit sanding sponge to smooth it down until you can’t feel the edge with your fingertips.  
* **Prime and Paint:** Don't skip the primer\! Drywall compound is "thirsty" and will soak up paint, leaving a dull spot if you don't prime first.

**26\. Interior Painting**

* **The Prep Work:** Wash the walls with a damp cloth and use painter’s tape to protect your baseboards, ceiling, and outlets.  
* **Cutting In:** Use a brush to paint a 2-inch border around the edges of the wall and around the trim where a roller can't reach.  
* **The "W" Technique:** Dip your roller and lay down a large "W" on the wall, then fill it in without lifting the roller to ensure even coverage.  
* **Light Layers:** Two thin coats are always better (and more professional) than one thick, gloopy coat that drips.  
* **Pull the Tape:** Remove the painter's tape while the paint is still slightly tacky to get those crisp, sharp lines we love.

**27\. Silencing a Squeaky Door**

* **Identify the Hinge:** Open and close the door slowly to find exactly which hinge is doing the "singing."  
* **Tap the Pin:** Use a hammer and a nail (or a screwdriver) to tap the hinge pin up about an inch—you don't have to take it all the way out.  
* **Lubricate:** Apply a drop of silicone spray, white lithium grease, or even a bit of dish soap to the pin.  
* **Work it In:** Tap the pin back down and swing the door back and forth several times to spread the lubricant through the hinge.  
* **Wipe the Excess:** Use a rag to catch any drips immediately so you don't stain your beautiful wood trim or carpet.

**28\. Caulking the Tub**

* **The Great Strip:** Use a caulk removal tool or a plastic scraper to pull up every bit of the old, moldy caulk.  
* **Disinfect:** Wipe the gap with rubbing alcohol or a bleach solution to kill any lingering mold spores so they don't grow under the new bead.  
* **The Weight Trick:** Fill the bathtub with water before you start; the weight "settles" the tub so the caulk won't crack when you take your first bath.  
* **The Smooth Bead:** Apply a continuous line of silicone caulk, then use a wet finger or a caulk tool to smooth it in one steady motion.  
* **The Long Dry:** Let it cure for at least 24 hours before you empty the water or use the shower—patience is a virtue\!

**29\. Replacing a Broken Tile**

* **Clear the Grout:** Use a grout saw or a rotary tool to carefully remove the grout around the broken tile so you don't crack the neighbors.  
* **The "X" Mark:** Use a hammer and center punch to gently tap a hole in the middle of the tile, then carefully pry the pieces up.  
* **Scrape the Floor:** Use a chisel to remove all the old, hardened adhesive from the floor until the surface is totally flat.  
* **Set the New Tile:** Apply "thin-set" mortar to the back of the new tile (buttering it like toast) and press it firmly into place.  
* **Grout and Clean:** After 24 hours, fill the gaps with matching grout, let it sit for 15 minutes, and wipe the haze off with a damp sponge.

### **Safety & Emergency**

**30\. Smoke & CO Detectors**

* **The Monthly Tap:** Press the "Test" button on every unit once a month; if it doesn't scream at you, it's not working.  
* **Annual Refresh:** Change the batteries every single year, even if they aren't chirping yet—pick a memorable date like New Year's.  
* **Vacuum the Dust:** Use a vacuum attachment to suck out dust and cobwebs from the vents; debris can cause annoying false alarms.  
* **Check the Date:** Flip the unit over to check the manufacture date; smoke detectors expire after 10 years, and CO detectors after 5–7.  
* **Strategic Placement:** Ensure you have at least one on every level of the home and specifically inside (or right outside) every bedroom.

**31\. Using a Fire Extinguisher (PASS)**

* **Pull the Pin:** Hold the extinguisher upright and pull the metal pin out to break the plastic tamper seal.  
* **Aim at the Base:** Point the nozzle or hose at the very bottom of the fire; aiming at the flames won't put out the fuel source.  
* **Squeeze the Lever:** Grip the handle firmly to release the extinguishing agent in a controlled burst.  
* **Sweep Side-to-Side:** Move the spray back and forth across the base of the fire until it appears completely extinguished.  
* **Monitor the Area:** Stay back and watch the spot for a few minutes to ensure the fire doesn't reignite from the heat.

**32\. Building an Emergency Kit**

* **Water & Food:** Stash one gallon of water per person per day and a 3-day supply of non-perishables (and a manual can opener\!).  
* **Light & Power:** Include a high-powered flashlight, a hand-crank radio, and a portable power bank for your phone.  
* **First Aid Plus:** Pack a standard medical kit but add a backup supply of any personal medications you take daily.  
* **The Paper Trail:** Keep copies of ID, insurance policies, and emergency contacts in a waterproof "go-bag."  
* **The Little Luxuries:** Toss in some extra cash, a deck of cards for boredom, and a warm emergency blanket.

**33\. Managing a Power Outage**

* **Unplug the Goodies:** Disconnect computers and TVs; when the power "surges" back on, it can fry your expensive electronics.  
* **Keep it Cold:** Open the fridge and freezer as little as possible—an unopened fridge keeps food safe for 4 hours; a freezer for 48\.  
* **Safe Lighting:** Use flashlights or LED lanterns rather than candles to avoid accidental fire "drama" in the dark.  
* **The "Wait" Period:** Once the lights flicker back on, wait 5–10 minutes before plugging things back in to ensure the grid is stable.  
* **Report the Issue:** Don't assume the neighbors called it in; notify your utility provider immediately so they know where the "dark zone" is.

**34\. Utility Contacts List**

* **Gather the Numbers:** Look up the emergency/outage lines for your electric, gas, and water companies.  
* **Print it Out:** Do not rely on your phone for this; write the numbers down on a physical card in case your battery dies.  
* **Include the Gas Line:** Know the specific "smell gas, act fast" number for your local natural gas provider.  
* **Post it Publicly:** Tape the list to the inside of a kitchen cabinet or near your breaker box where everyone can see it.  
* **Add Local Heroes:** Include the non-emergency numbers for your local police and fire departments for quick access.

### **Appliances**

**35\. Cleaning Fridge Coils**

* **Pull it Out:** Gently shimmy the fridge away from the wall (it's a great workout\!) and unplug it for safety.  
* **Locate the Coils:** Depending on your model, the coils are either a black grid on the back or hidden behind a plate at the bottom front.  
* **Vacuum Away:** Use a narrow vacuum attachment to suck up the "dust bunnies" that make the motor work overtime.  
* **Brush the Grime:** Use a coil brush or a long, soft brush to loosen the stubborn hair and lint between the loops.  
* **Restore and Plug:** Push it back into place, ensuring you aren't pinching the water line or the cord, and plug it back in.

**36\. Clearing the Dryer Vent**

* **Unplug and Detach:** Pull the dryer out and disconnect the large silver "accordion" duct from the back of the machine.  
* **Vacuum the Tube:** Use a vacuum to reach into the back of the dryer and as far into the wall duct as you can.  
* **The Snake Method:** Use a dryer vent cleaning brush (a long flexible rod) to scrub the entire length of the duct leading outside.  
* **Check the Exterior:** Go outside and make sure the vent flap opens freely and isn't blocked by lint or bird nests.  
* **Reattach Securely:** Clamp the duct back on tightly; a loose vent leaks hot, moist air (and lint\!) behind your walls.

**37\. Dishwasher Maintenance**

* **Clean the Filter:** Remove the cylindrical filter at the bottom, rinse it under hot water, and scrub it with a soft brush to remove "food funk."  
* **Clear the Arms:** Use a toothpick to poke out any seeds or hard water deposits clogging the tiny holes in the spray arms.  
* **The Vinegar Wash:** Place a bowl of white vinegar on the top rack and run a hot cycle to dissolve grease and lime scale.  
* **The Baking Soda Finish:** Sprinkle a cup of baking soda on the bottom and run one more short cycle to deodorize and brighten.  
* **Wipe the Seal:** Clean the rubber gasket around the door with a damp cloth to prevent leaks and moldy smells.

**38\. Garbage Disposal Care**

* **Ice and Salt:** Toss in a handful of ice cubes and a half-cup of kosher salt; run it with cold water to "scrub" the blades.  
* **Citrus Refresh:** Grind up a few lemon or orange peels to neutralize odors and leave your sink smelling like a spa.  
* **The Brush Down:** Use a long-handled scrub brush to clean the underside of the black rubber splash guard—that's where the slime hides\!  
* **Cold Water Only:** Always run *cold* water when using the disposal; hot water melts grease, which then clogs your pipes further down.  
* **No "Hard" Stuff:** Never put bones, pits, or fibrous things like celery down there—they are the ultimate disposal mood-killers.

**39\. The Appliance Deep Clean**

* **The Oven Ritual:** Remove the racks and soak them in soapy water; use a paste of baking soda and water on the oven floor overnight.  
* **Microwave Steam:** Heat a bowl of water and lemon slices for 5 minutes; the steam softens the "splatters" so they wipe right off.  
* **Washing Machine Refresh:** Run an empty hot cycle with a cup of bleach or a specialized cleaner to kill "gym clothes" bacteria.  
* **Under-Appliance Sweep:** Use a flat duster to reach under the stove and dishwasher; you'd be shocked at what collects under there.  
* **The Exterior Shine:** Finish by wiping down all handles and control panels with a microfiber cloth to remove those pesky fingerprints.
